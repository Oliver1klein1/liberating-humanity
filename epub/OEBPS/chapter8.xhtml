Chapter 8:
Did Jesus Endorse Yahweh's Law?

"Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them. For truly I tell you, until heaven and earth disappear, not the smallest letter, not the least stroke of a pen, will by any means disappear from the Law until everything is accomplished."

— Matthew 5:17-19 (NIV)

You may be thinking: "Wait. Hold on. How do you reconcile this? Jesus quotes Old Testament passages that reference Yahweh. And what about Matthew 5:17-19? Doesn't Jesus explicitly affirm the Mosaic Law?"

These are valid questions. And they deserve honest answers. Because if Jesus genuinely endorsed Yahweh's commands—the very ones that demanded genocide, child sacrifice, and rivers of blood—then everything we've explored in this book collapses. But here's the thing: Jesus' actions throughout his life flatly contradict those words.

It seems likely to me—more than likely, obvious—that Jesus' words were twisted to appear to support Yahweh's practices and commands. He clearly called Yahweh evil (as we saw in Chapter 1), so Yahweh's commands would have evil baked in. Large parts of the New Testament were written to support these evil practices, including blood sacrifice, so the NT writers—supported by the powers of the day (empire-supported religious institutions)—would not have left texts unaltered where Jesus seemed to go against those practices.

Jesus would never condone evil laws. And it's fairly obvious that some of these laws were evil and designed to horrify—Yahweh himself admitted as much:

"I defiled them through their gifts—the sacrifice of every firstborn—that I might fill them with horror so they would know that I am Yahweh." (Ezekiel 20:25-26, NIV)

There are words ascribed to Jesus which make it clear to me that evil human hearts were involved in their creation. For example, when Jesus talks about how the Father forgives freely, he would never qualify that by saying "except for this one instance where he will never forgive you" (the so-called unpardonable sin in Matthew 12:31-32). Similarly, he would never say "these evil laws will last forever and not one jot or tittle of them is going to ever disappear." That sounds like somebody using the brilliant strategy of putting words into Jesus' mouth to preserve the sacrificial system (not least because numerous 'middlemen' were on the take, financially, for starters.)

So when I read words like "not one jot or tittle of the Law will pass away" and note that for centuries those words have been read as proof that Jesus endorsed the Mosaic code, I chuckle quietly. Because every action of His life… healing on the Sabbath, forgiving without sacrifice, touching the unclean, defending adulterers… flatly denies it.

Jesus' Actions vs. Yahweh's Law

Yahweh's Law Demanded

  • Death for Sabbath breaking (Numbers 15:35)
  • Stoning for adultery (Leviticus 20:10)
  • Sacrifice for forgiveness (Leviticus 1:4)
  • Avoidance of the unclean (Leviticus 13:45-46)
  • Blood payment for sin (Leviticus 17:11)

Jesus' Actions

  • Healed on the Sabbath (Mark 3:1-6)
  • Refused to stone the adulteress (John 8:1-11)
  • Forgave without sacrifice (Mark 2:5; Luke 23:34)
  • Touched lepers (Mark 1:41)
  • Died asking for forgiveness, not demanding payment

When Jesus said "You have heard it said, 'Eye for eye,' but I say to you, do not resist an evil person" (Matthew 5:38-39), he is overturning Torah. His use of Yahwist language is not loyalty; it is confrontation. The Gospels preserve traces of this inversion, though later editors softened the blows to keep Jesus inside the Yahweh framework.

"You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.' But I tell you, do not resist an evil person. If anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other cheek also." (Matthew 5:38-39, NIV)

This is not affirmation. This is annihilation. Jesus doesn't say, "The Law is good, but let's add grace to it." He says, "You heard the old way. Here's the new way. Turn the other cheek." He's not building on Yahweh's foundation—he's replacing it.

Consider how Jesus handled another of Yahweh's commands—divorce. The Law of Moses permitted it (Deuteronomy 24:1-4). But Jesus?

"It was because your hearts were hard that Moses wrote you this law," Jesus replied. "But at the beginning of creation God 'made them male and female.' 'For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh.' So they are no longer two, but one flesh. Therefore what God has joined together, let no one separate." (Mark 10:5-9, NIV)

Jesus doesn't say, "The Law allows divorce, so it's fine." He says, "Moses only wrote that law because of your hard hearts. That's not how it was supposed to be." He's not affirming the Law—he's exposing its inadequacy.

The "Jot and Tittle" Problem

The phrase about the Law enduring "until heaven and earth pass away" (Matthew 5:17-19) was likely not even uttered by Jesus at all. But even if it was, it likely defended Jesus' moral law—love of God and neighbor—not the ritual code of blood and retribution which even Yahweh called "not good" and intended to "horrify."

"I gave them other statutes that were not good and laws through which they could not live; I defiled them through their gifts—the sacrifice of every firstborn—that I might fill them with horror so they would know that I am Yahweh." (Ezekiel 20:25-26, NIV)

Yahweh admitted that his laws were designed to horrify. Yet somehow, we're supposed to believe Jesus endorsed them? That makes no sense.

A Critical Insight: Matthew as Counter-Paul Text

Early church voices such as Augustine admitted that James, Jude, and 2 Peter were written to correct misreadings of Paul's "faith-alone" doctrine. The Gospel of Matthew, in turn, served as a counter-Paul text—written to reassert the importance of Jesus' teachings in the face of Pauline theology that was sweeping the early church. This context matters. Matthew may have preserved some authentic Jesus material, but it also contains insertions designed to counter Paul's message and reinforce obedience to Jesus' teachings.

Here's where it gets subtly brilliant: Even the word "least" (Greek elachistos, Latin paulus) may have been a deliberate pun. Consider Matthew 5:19:

"Anyone who breaks one of the least of these commands and teaches others to do the same will be called least in the kingdom of heaven." (Matthew 5:19, NIV)

Jesus' warning, then, may have anticipated a future teacher who would annul the moral law under the banner of grace. "Whoever relaxes the commandments will be called Paulus, the least, in the Kingdom." The name itself becomes a warning. Whether this was intentional wordplay or the writer of Matthew pointing fingers directly at Paul, the connection is interesting, to say the least.

But there's more. Notice what Jesus says about the greatest commandment:

"'Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.' This is the first and greatest commandment. And the second is like it: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.' All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments." (Matthew 22:37-40, NIV)

If all the Law and the Prophets hang on love, then any law that contradicts love—any command that demands violence, retribution, or bloodshed—cannot be part of the Law Jesus meant to preserve. He's not preserving Yahweh's entire code; he's dissolving them with the brilliant light of the only real power: love.

The Unpardonable Sin Contradiction

Consider another contradiction: Jesus consistently taught that the Father forgives freely, unconditionally, without limits:

"Be merciful, just as your Father is merciful." (Luke 6:36, NIV)

"If you forgive other people when they sin against you, your heavenly Father will also forgive you." (Matthew 6:14, NIV)

Yet in Matthew 12:31-32, we find this:

"And so I tell you, every kind of sin and slander can be forgiven, but blasphemy against the Spirit will not be forgiven. Anyone who speaks a word against the Son of Man will be forgiven, but anyone who speaks against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven, either in this age or in the age to come." (Matthew 12:31-32, NIV)

This doesn't sound like Jesus. The man who forgave his killers while dying, who told parables of prodigals welcomed home, who said to forgive "seventy times seven" (Matthew 18:22)… would he really carve out one exception where forgiveness is impossible?

This sounds like scribal insertion—a way to control those who questioned emerging orthodoxies, to threaten anyone who dared challenge the new theological systems being built around Jesus' name. Fear is a powerful tool for maintaining religious authority.

Paul's Preservation of the Sacrificial System

Here's where it gets subtly brilliant: Paul's message appears to cancel the Law… "you are not under the Law but under grace" (Romans 6:14)… yet it simultaneously rescues the very system Jesus condemned.

"For sin shall not be your master, because you are not under the law, but under grace." (Romans 6:14, NIV)

Once the temple fell in 70 CE, animal sacrifice was impossible. Paul's theology solved that crisis: keep the blood-economy alive by making Jesus the final, perpetual offering. The slaughter continues symbolically. No lambs, no smoke… just a human scapegoat enthroned as divine payment.

Outwardly the Law dies; inwardly its cruel logic survives. What looks like liberation is camouflage for preservation. In this light, Paul's "abolition" of the Law is not reform but a conversion of medium: the temple moves into the human conscience, the altar into the mind. People still kneel before AN ANGRY GOD DEMANDING BLOOD… they simply believe the debt was prepaid.

The Temple Economy: Before and After 70 CE

Before 70 CE:

  • Physical temple in Jerusalem
  • Animal sacrifices daily
  • Priests collecting offerings
  • Blood flowing from altars
  • Guilt requiring physical payment

After 70 CE (Paul's Solution):

  • Spiritual temple in hearts
  • Jesus as perpetual sacrifice
  • Belief as currency
  • Blood memorialized in communion
  • Guilt requiring spiritual payment

When the smoke cleared from Rome's siege of Jerusalem in 70 CE, the altar of Yahweh lay in ruins. For the first time in a millennium, no lamb bled on the Temple stones. Religion faced a problem: how could sin still be paid for if the machinery of sacrifice was gone?

The temple economy had always depended on guilt. Priests needed sinners; sinners needed priests; both needed a sacrificial exchange to keep GOD appeased. Paul's letters appeared just in time to save that logic. He replaced the visible altar with a theological one. The lamb became THE LAMB. The ritual continued invisibly, now internalized through belief.

Paul's genius… or deception… was to cloak continuity in the language of change.

Paul's Theological Sleight of Hand:

  • He proclaimed, "We are no longer under the Law," while importing its central premise: sin requires blood.
  • He abolished circumcision yet kept the knife's theology.
  • Instead of offering an animal, believers now "partake of the body and blood of Christ." (1 Corinthians 11:24-25)
  • The vocabulary shifted from RITUAL to A 'REMEMBRANCE' RITUAL, but the energy feeding Yahweh's appetite remained the same.

Thus the old covenant was not canceled… it was digitized, uploaded into human consciousness: Sacrificial System 2.0. The smoke of burnt flesh became the smoke of guilt and gratitude rising in prayer. What looked like progress was simply THE LAW WEARING A NEW SKIN.

By rebranding bloodshed as "grace," Paul made the cruelty appear compassionate.

"For you know that it was not with perishable things such as silver or gold that you were redeemed… but with the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or defect." (1 Peter 1:18-19, NIV)

Instead of you dying, someone dies for you. The transaction still requires death; it just moves the pain one step away from the worshiper.

The result was a gentler vocabulary concealing THE SAME VIOLENCE. People still kneel to an angry deity; they simply believe His wrath had already been vented on His Son. And so guilt endured, sacrifice endured, and love remained conditional… only now sanctified as orthodoxy.

Jesus had tried to end that system forever. He unveiled a Father who needed no payment, whose forgiveness was the air itself:

"Your Father knows what you need before you ask him." (Matthew 6:8, NIV)

"But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be children of your Father in heaven. He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous." (Matthew 5:44-45, NIV)

No conditions. No prerequisites. No blood required. Just love. Just mercy. Just forgiveness flowing like water.

Paul's "gospel" repackaged the old deal with a prettier bow. The altar was never dismantled; it was just relocated… into human imagination, where it could operate indefinitely without the cost of lambs or incense.

The Core Contradiction:

If Jesus truly meant that "not one jot or tittle" of the Law would pass away, why did he:

  • Heal on the Sabbath when the Law demanded death for Sabbath-breaking? (Numbers 15:35; Mark 3:1-6)
  • Forgive without sacrifice when the Law demanded blood for atonement? (Leviticus 17:11; Mark 2:5)
  • Touch lepers when the Law demanded separation from the unclean? (Leviticus 13:45-46; Mark 1:41)
  • Refuse to stone an adulteress when the Law demanded execution? (Leviticus 20:10; John 8:1-11)
  • Tell parables where the Father forgives before repentance is even sought? (Luke 15:11-32)

Either Jesus was the greatest hypocrite who ever lived, or those words were inserted by those with an agenda. Given everything else we know about Jesus, which explanation makes more sense?

Jesus' True "Law"

If Jesus did speak about a Law that would never pass away, it wasn't Yahweh's Mosaic code. It was the law of love:

"A new command I give you: Love one another. As I have loved you, so you must love one another." (John 13:34, NIV)

"So in everything, do to others what you would have them do to you, for this sums up the Law and the Prophets." (Matthew 7:12, NIV)

This is the Law that endures. Not the ritual code. Not the blood sacrifices. Not the death penalties. Just love.

So when you encounter passages where Jesus seems to quote the Old Testament or affirm Yahweh's commands, look deeper. When he uses Yahwist language, he's often subverting it, not endorsing it. When he references the Law, he's pointing to a higher law—the law of love that transcends ritual and retribution.

Jesus didn't come to preserve Yahweh's system. He came to replace it. And every action of his life proves it.

The Real Law Jesus Endorsed:

  • "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind." (Matthew 22:37)
  • "Love your neighbor as yourself." (Matthew 22:39)
  • "Love your enemies." (Matthew 5:44)
  • "Do to others what you would have them do to you." (Matthew 7:12)
  • "Be merciful, just as your Father is merciful." (Luke 6:36)

This is the Law that will never pass away. Everything else—the sacrifices, the penalties, the blood—was designed to horrify and control. Jesus came to set us free from all of it.
